Part 1.1

You can use some simple kernels in order to get great insights of images. Two examples we explore in this part are the horizontal and vertical edge kernels. The images below show the original image of the cameraman, the horizontal derivative, vertical derivative, and the gradient magnitude in respective orders. The gradient magnitude can be calculated by using the formula \(\sqrt{\frac{df}{dx}^2 + \frac{df}{dy}^2}\).This gives a combination of both horizontal and vertical edges to (theoretically) give all edges in an image. The threshold for the gradient magnitude was hand-tuned. I found filtering out any pixels below 0.178 in magnitude gave a pretty good result.

Part 1.2

Sometimes it's better to actually blur an image slightly with a gaussian kernel before looking for edges. This allows us to remove some noise and other bad artifacts before we take the partial derivatives. This ideally gives a better result for finding edges in a picture. We can see that the partial derivatives seem more "clean" in a sense, and better resemble the actual visible edges seen in the original picture and are also much "stronger" than part 1.1. We also see less noise in the gradient magnitude of the image in the grass and clearer edges. The handpicked threshold was slightly different: 0.13

Part 1.3 - Image Straightening

One can also straighten images by using the \(\frac{df}{dx}\) and \(\frac{df}{dy}\) derivatives of a picture. This can be done by using the formula \(\arctan \theta = \frac{-\frac{df}{dy}}{\frac{df}{dx}} \). One can apply this after getting the derivatives of an image. This gives the angles at every point in an image, and we can then put this in a histogram. One thing to keep in mind is this gives the angle in radians, so we must convert back to degrees multiplying by \(\frac{180}{\pi}\). We can look at the histogram and see which angle appears most often. It's likely that the angle which appears most often is either vertical or horizontal. So for example of the bin at 3 degrees is largest, than the image is probably tilted by 3 degrees. We can make this a little more sophisticated by checking bins that are 90 degrees apart as well in order to capture the full essence of vertical and horizontal edges. So for example, I look at bins at -89 degrees, 1 degrees, and 91 degrees. Then I check bins at -88, 2, and 92 and so on. I assume that tilts are less than 20 degrees in order to boost efficiency of my algorithm. Some results are shown below.

Part 2.1 - Image Sharpening

Part 2.2 Hybrid Images

We can also merge images by combining ones high frequencies and anothers low frequencies to make some wacky results! First you have to align the images (which the code was thankfully provided for) and then you get the high frequencies of one image, low frequencies of another, and combine them! Then one image should appear when close, and a separate one should appear when far away! That's because when close up you will see the high frequencies, and farther away you will see the lower frequencies.

Reflections

The most important thing I learned in this project is that sometimes your algorithm can work exactly how you want it, but the images you're testing them out on just will not cooperate. For example, when I was testing my straightening algorithm on images, I would choose pictures that appeared, to me, very obviously skewed. However, the histogram showing the calculated angles in the picture would overwhelming be binned at 0 degrees to my frustration. I was indeed choosing the right bin, but the angles themselves were wrong. That signaled to me that the proposed algorithm may be a little too simple for some more complex pictures. The same thing happened for my merging (with the egg and face). I think my algorithm works fine, but it is really dependent on what images I use it on. It's really really difficult to make a great algorithm that works for a large general dataset, and I think it's somewhat acceptable for algorithms to have failures as long as there still exists an application for them. So I will celebrate my sucesses for this project, and will not be too sad about the failures while still keeping them in mind.
